Introduction

Serie A Scout Index
Complete Methodology Guide

A data-driven model for evaluating Serie A players using independent performance metrics — corrected for opponent difficulty, stabilized with Bayesian Shrinkage, standardized with Winsorized z-scores and validated with Bootstrap confidence intervals.

Why this model is different: Most ranking systems use raw goal/assist counts — heavily influenced by luck and team quality. TPI measures the process, not the outcome: expected value created per 90 minutes, weighted by opponent strength, stabilized for sample size, and validated against future performance.
1Overview

The TPI Pipeline

From raw FBref data to final TPI — 8 steps, each statistically justified.

1
FBref data (Opta source)
xG, xA, goals, minutes per match. Opta — the most reliable public data source for professional football.
2
SOS — Strength of Schedule
Mean xG conceded by each opponent. Players who faced stronger defenses get a bonus multiplier — fairness across fixtures.
3
SOS-weighted averages
All offensive metrics weighted by 1/SOS. One goal against Napoli counts more than one against the bottom team.
4
4 core dimensions + Bayesian Shrinkage
Output Adj/90, Centrality, Team Boost, Consistency — across 5 contexts. Adaptive K shrinks estimates for low-sample players.
5
Winsorized z-score standardization
Top/bottom 5% of values trimmed before computing μ and σ. Prevents one extreme outlier from distorting the entire league distribution.
6
TPI = mean of 4 z-scores
All dimensions equally weighted after standardization. Missing Boost handled gracefully — mean of 3 remaining dimensions used.
7
AII + PRI (TPI Pro)
Optional. Age Impact Index and Physical Reliability Index added as z-scores — TPI Pro = mean of 6 dimensions.
8
Confidence Score
Geometric mean of minutes played ratio and dimension completeness. Tells you how much to trust each player's TPI estimate.
2Dati Base

xG — Expected Goals

La probabilità che un tiro diventi goal, calcolata da Opta su milioni di tiri storici.

Come viene calcolato

Opta costruisce un modello ML addestrato su ogni tiro della storia del calcio professionistico:
  • Distanza e angolo dalla porta
  • Tipo di tiro — piede destro, sinistro, testa
  • Tipo di azione — cross, corner, calco piazzato, contropiede
  • Pressione dei difensori al momento del tiro

xA — Expected Assists

La stessa logica di xG applicata ai passaggi che precedono un tiro.

xA di un passaggio = xG del tiro che ne è seguito
xA conta anche se...
  • Il compagno manca la porta
  • Il portiere para brillantemente
  • C'è un rimbalzo intermedio
Assist ufficiale conta solo se...
  • Il goal viene segnato

3Dimensione 1

Output Offensivo Adj/90

La produzione offensiva per 90 minuti, corretta per la difficoltà degli avversari.

Output Adj/90 = (xG + xA) / minuti × 90 / SOS
Passo 1: Somma xG + xA di tutte le partite nel contesto.
Passo 2: Normalizza per 90 minuti → confrontabile tra minutaggi diversi.
Passo 3: Dividi per SOS → correggi per la difficoltà degli avversari.
Stesso output grezzo, valore diverso:
Giocatore A: (xG+xA)/90 = 0.45, SOS = 0.85 → Output Adj = 0.53
Giocatore B: (xG+xA)/90 = 0.45, SOS = 1.20 → Output Adj = 0.38
Stesso dato grezzo, ma B ha giocato contro difese più deboli — il TPI lo riconosce.
4Dimensione 2

Centralità Offensiva

Quanta parte della produzione offensiva della squadra passa per questo giocatore — stabilizzata con Bayesian shrinkage.

Centralità = (xG_ind + xA_ind + K × prior) / (xG_squadra + K) × 100
// prior ≈ 20% (media ATT+CEN lega) · K = 12 (peso del prior)
// K_ctx = K_base × (n_tot / n_ctx) per contesti con pochi dati

Effetto Bayesian Shrinkage — convergenza

Con pochi dati la stima bayesiana è vicina al prior (20%). Con molte partite converge verso il valore osservato reale (45%).
Convergenza bayesiana — osservato=45%, prior=20%, K=12
Stima Bayesiana
Osservato 45%
Prior lega 20%
K=12 soglia
Perché K = 12? Corrisponde a circa 12 partite — il punto in cui le stime calcistiche diventano statisticamente affidabili. Con K troppo basso: instabile (2 partite eccezionali → 80%). Con K troppo alto: tutti convergono al prior. Per acquisti invernali: K × 1.8.

Età Index AII

Indice composito che misura il valore del giocatore considerando il ciclo di carriera — picco prestativo, esperienza accumulata e freschezza fisica. Usato nel TPI Pro come dimensione opzionale.

Componenti della formula

AII = 0.50 × PeakProximity + 0.30 × ExperienceFactor + 0.20 × Freshness

PeakProximity = exp(−0.5 × ((età − 27) / 4.5)²) // Gaussiana, picco a 27 anni
ExperienceFactor = min(1.0, (età − 17) / 10.0) // satura dopo ~10 anni di carriera
Freshness = 1.0 se età ≤ 27, altrimenti max(0, 1 − (età−27)×0.05) // −5%/anno

Affidabilità Fisica PRI

Misura quanto un giocatore è fisicamente presente e affidabile — basato su disponibilità reale, frequenza degli infortuni e gravità delle assenze. Richiede la tabella t_infortuni nel DB.

Formula

PRI = 0.50 × Disponibilità + 0.30 × InjuryFree + 0.20 × SeverityOk

Disponibilità = partite_giocate / partite_disponibili_stagione
InjuryFree = max(0, 1 − n_infortuni × 0.15) // −15% per ogni infortunio
SeverityOk = max(0, 1 − giorni_out / 90) // −1.1%/giorno fuori (cap a 90 gg)

7Statistics

Winsorized z-score

The transformation that lets us combine metrics with completely different scales — made robust against outliers.

Step 1: Winsorize → clip values to [p5, p95] before computing μ and σ
Step 2: z = (value − μ_winsorized) / σ_winsorized
Step 3: clip z to [−3, +3]
// Reference population: ATT + CEN qualificati (≥ minuti soglia)
❌ Standard z-score (old)
A player with 1.2 xG/90 in 2 matches inflates σ for the entire league. Result: everyone else's z-score is compressed — rankings distorted.
✅ Winsorized z-score (new)
Top/bottom 5% trimmed before computing μ and σ. Outlier still scores +3.0, but doesn't distort the distribution for everyone else.

Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

Every correlation reported in the validation (Pearson r, Spearman ρ) includes a 95% Bootstrap CI computed with 1000 resamples (seed=42). This tells you the plausible range of the true correlation — not just the point estimate.
For each of 1000 iterations:
  → sample n players with replacement
  → compute correlation r*
CI = [percentile(r*, 2.5%), percentile(r*, 97.5%)]
Example: Pearson r = 0.47 with 95% CI [0.31, 0.61] means we're 95% confident the true correlation is between 0.31 and 0.61 — even if we collected different data. A wide CI signals small sample; a narrow CI signals stability.

TPI & TPI Pro

The final index. TPI Classic uses 4 dimensions. TPI Pro extends to 6 when age and injury data are available.

TPI = mean(z_output_adj, z_centralità, z_boost, z_consistenza)
// if Boost N/A → mean of 3 remaining · computed for 5 contexts

TPI Pro = mean(z_output_adj, z_centralità, z_boost, z_consistenza, z_aii, z_pri)
// z_aii and z_pri optional — excluded if missing data

IQR-based Consistency (upgraded)

The classic CV formula (1 − σ/μ) breaks when μ → 0 and amplifies extreme values. We replaced it with an IQR-based robust consistency:
Consistency = 1 − IQR / (median + ε)
// IQR = Q75 − Q25 · more resistant to outliers than σ/μ
❌ Old: CV = σ/μ
If μ = 0.05 and σ = 0.04 → CV = 0.80 → Consistency = 0.20
But if μ = 0.05 and σ = 0.051 → Consistency = −0.02 → negative!
✅ New: IQR-based
Always bounded [0, 1]. Robust to a single outlier match. A player with 8 steady games and 1 explosion is rated more consistently than with CV.

TPI Confidence Score

A new 0–1 score that tells you how much to trust each player's TPI estimate.
Confidence = √(minutes_ratio × dimension_completeness)
// geometric mean — both factors must be high for high confidence
Example: A player with 1800 minutes and all 4 dimensions = Confidence 0.91. A player with 400 minutes = Confidence ≤ 0.55 regardless of how good the TPI looks. Use confidence to filter out noise in early-season rankings.

Form EWMA — Forma Recente

La media mobile esponenzialmente pesata con α = 0.30.

form_t = α × output_t + (1 − α) × form_{t-1}
// α = 0.30 → 30% peso alla partita recente, 70% allo storico

Form Trend = (EWMA_ultima / media_stagionale) − 1
Perché α = 0.30? α=0.50 reagisce troppo ai singoli picchi — segnale rumoroso. α=0.10 troppo lento — non cattura i veri cambi di forma. α=0.30 è il compromesso standard in letteratura sportiva (usato da FBref e StatsBomb).

AValidation

Test A — Pearson r + Spearman ρ + Bootstrap CI

Three complementary statistics to measure how well TPI correlates with Fantacalcio expert ratings.

Pearson r = Σ[(xi−x̄)(yi−ȳ)] / [n·σx·σy]
// linear correlation · sensitive to outliers

Spearman ρ = Pearson r applied to ranks
// rank correlation · robust to outliers · better for sports data

Bootstrap 95% CI: resample 1000× → [p2.5, p97.5] of r*
// tells you the plausible range, not just the point estimate
Why both Pearson and Spearman?
Pearson assumes normality and linearity. Spearman only requires monotonic relationship. When they're close (e.g. r=0.47 vs ρ=0.45), the relationship is genuinely linear — more reliable. When they diverge, outliers are driving the Pearson result.
Cohen's d — Effect Size
Compares Fantacalcio ratings between top 25% and bottom 25% TPI players. d > 0.8 = large effect. d = 0.5 = medium. d < 0.2 = negligible. This tells you if TPI separation actually translates into rating differences.

CValidation

Test C — Spearman ρ + Kendall τ + RMSE

Three metrics for the predictive backtest. Each captures a different aspect of predictive stability.

Spearman ρ = Pearson r on ranks
// robust to outliers · does early ranking predict late ranking?

Kendall τ = (concordant − discordant pairs) / total pairs
// more conservative than Spearman · better with small n · preferred in academic literature

RMSE = √mean((predicted − actual)²)
MAE = mean(|predicted − actual|)
// absolute error in original units · tells you how wrong the model is in practice
Kendall τ vs Spearman ρ
Spearman is easier to compute but more sensitive to tied ranks. Kendall τ is more conservative — it counts how many pairs of players have the same relative order in both halves of the season. If τ = 0.55, 77.5% of player pairs maintained their relative quality order.
Why RMSE + MAE matter
A correlation of 0.70 tells you direction, not magnitude of error. RMSE and MAE translate predictive power into practical terms: if RMSE = 0.08 xG/90, that's the average prediction error in real units. MAE is more interpretable; RMSE penalizes large errors more.

Backtest — First half → Second half of season

Interpretation guide: Spearman ρ ≥ 0.65 = strong predictive stability. Kendall τ ≥ 0.45 = most player pairs maintain relative order. RMSE / MAE in context: compare to the standard deviation of output/90 in the league — if RMSE < 0.5×SD, the model has real predictive value.
Bottom line: A model that can predict second-half quality from first-half TPI is capturing real, stable player quality — not just noise or hot streaks. This is the most important validation test for scouting applications.
